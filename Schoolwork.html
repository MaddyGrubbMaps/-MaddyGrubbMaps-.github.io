My Montana State University Journey

I decided to come to Montana State due it's proximity to the mountains, and wonderful opportunity to run trails and climb peaks right in our backyard. I have pursued a bachelors of science in Physical Geography and GIS, and am graduating in the summer of 2022.

Not only have I learned alot about myself during my time here, but I have also developed a broad understanding of Earth Sciences, and have had the opportunity to be apart of many incredble field, and geospatial based projects in many fields.

I came to school in 2018, fresh off the Pacific Crest Trail, with a basic understanding of open source map-making using a combinatiaon of tools in QGIS, Blender and Adobe. I have been able to further all these skills, incorporate in depth analysis to my maps, and work on projects in ArcGIS Pro, Mapbox, Story Maps, and have picked up a little bit of python and coding. I've been able to meet some incredible mentors here, (and out on the trails!) who have encouraged me in my education and arreer. 

My main goal with mapping, and part of the reason why I love it so much is I get to connect science, art and emotion, through a medium totally unlike any other. Cartographic possibilites are endless, and the art of data visualization through maps is constantly evolving.

Research Papers & Projects

HAMILTON  MT - A Place Based Historical Review

The paper takes a deep dive into the known history of people and land use change in the Bitterroot Valley. Starting with the Glacial Lake Missoula Floods ~ 15,000 years ago, touching on the stroy of infectious disease in the area and ending with the current state of societal and economic drivers.

Written as a midterm paper for Geographic Thought - Spring 22

Read the Hamilton Paper

PICO DE ORIZABA - Mountain Geography of Mexico's Tallest Peak

A conclusive overview of the characteristics that make this volcano so special. Covers geoglogy, hydrology, topography, ecology & vegetation patterns, human impacts, and climate.

Written as a final paper for Mountain Geography - Fall 21

Read the Pico De Orizaba Paper

SNOW DEPTH VARIABILITY OF WIND LOADED SLOPES

This snow sicence based research project, combined field and GIS methods to model snow depths at the slope scale. Data points were collected on site at the Yellowstone Club and interpolated to create estimated surface maps of 2 study plots. The data created is pertinent to a Masters project being conduted by Nata De Leeuw.

Final Project for Snow Dynamics & Accumulation
